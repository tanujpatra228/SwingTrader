# Step 4 — Read the Charts

This is where you look at each remaining stock one by one and decide: is this stock in a good position to buy soon?

Open TradingView. Search each stock name. Make sure you are on the **daily chart** (click "1D"). Add the AP AI1 indicator (search it, add it once — it stays).

For each stock, answer two questions.

---

## Question 1: Is this stock going up overall?

Look at the chart. Cover the right half with your hand and look at the overall shape:

- Does the price generally move from the **bottom-left to the top-right**? → Good ✓
- Is it going sideways for many months with no clear direction? → Skip
- Is it falling from top-left to bottom-right? → Skip immediately

Now look at the coloured lines (EMAs):

- Price above the **green line** (50 EMA)? → Uptrend confirmed ✓
- Price below the green line? → Skip this stock

If the stock fails Question 1, stop here and move to the next stock. Do not look for reasons to keep it.

---

## Question 2: Is it resting quietly right now?

The best time to buy is NOT when a stock is flying up. It is when a stock has gone up, then stopped and rested for a while, and is about to move up again.

Look at the most recent 2–4 weeks of the chart. A good resting pattern looks like:

**What you want to see:**
- The stock rose strongly a few weeks ago (big green candles)
- Since then, it has been moving sideways in a tight, narrow band
- The daily price moves are small — candles are short, not long
- The volume bars at the bottom are **getting smaller** during this rest (means people are not selling, just holding quietly)

This quiet sideways rest is called a **base** or **tight range.** It means: sellers have stopped selling, and the stock is building up energy for the next move.

**What you do NOT want to see:**
- The stock is still falling
- The price is jumping up and down wildly (big swings both ways)
- The stock just ran up 15–20% in the last 2–3 days — it is now extended, wait for it to rest before buying

---

## Good base vs bad base (Ankur's key distinction)

Not all resting periods are equal. There are two types:

**Linear base (the good one):**
- The stock goes up in a straight, steady manner — each small move upward leads to another
- During the rest, the candles are small and consistent
- The price does not swing wildly in either direction
- Looks like a clean, tidy sideways band on the chart

**Choppy base (avoid or be very careful):**
- The stock moves up sharply, then falls sharply, then up again — no clean direction
- During the rest, the candles are large and erratic
- The price swings from very high to very low within the resting zone
- Looks jagged and messy on the chart

**Rule: prefer linear bases. Choppy bases break down more often and the move after them is less reliable.** If you cannot tell which type it is, skip the stock. There will always be more setups.

---

## What a good setup looks like (in simple words)

Imagine a ball being held underwater. The longer and more quietly it stays down, the higher it bounces when released. A stock that has risen, then rested tightly for 2–4 weeks, is like that ball.

Strong rise → quiet sideways rest (tight range, low volume) → that's your setup.

---

## Which stage is the stock in? (Ankur's 3 momentum types)

Not all stocks in an uptrend are equally worth buying. Before adding a stock to your watchlist, identify which stage it is in:

**Stage 1 — Emerging momentum:**
- Stock just broke out of a base for the first time today or yesterday
- Big green candle, high volume — the move just started
- Do NOT buy immediately. Add to radar. Wait for it to rest and form a new tight range (3–7 days of small moves). Then watch for the next breakout.

**Stage 2 — Established momentum (this is where you make the most money):**
- Stock already moved up from its base, had one pullback or consolidation, and is now forming another tight range
- Trend is confirmed. Structure is clean. Volume contracted during rest.
- This is your primary target. Enter at EMA pullback or next breakout.

**Stage 3 — Late stage (avoid for new positions):**
- Stock already ran 70–100%+ from its original base
- Multiple flags already done (3rd or 4th consolidation)
- Risk-reward no longer works. Volatility is high. Stop-loss will be far.
- After a late-stage stock peaks: expect 8–12 weeks of messy consolidation, 20–40% correction, and the 20 EMA getting cut multiple times. Only tradeable again after a fresh base forms.

**Simple rule: build your watchlist from Stage 1 (radar only) and Stage 2 (active entries). Remove Stage 3.**

---

## Sorting your list

After checking all 5–12 stocks, sort them into two groups:

**Watchlist (add these):**
- Going up overall ✓
- Above green line (50 EMA) ✓
- Currently in a tight, quiet rest ✓

**Skip for now:**
- Falling, or below EMAs
- Moving too wildly
- Just ran up a lot and not rested yet (wait for it to come back later)

You will usually end up with 2–5 good watchlist stocks. That is plenty.

---

## One more thing: sector check

If multiple stocks from the same sector (e.g., 3 banking stocks) show up in your watchlist, that's actually a good sign — it means the whole sector is strong. Prefer stocks from sectors that are currently leading the market.

To quickly check: look at the Momentum scan results from Step 2. Which sectors appear most? Those are the leading sectors. Try to pick your watchlist stocks from those sectors.
