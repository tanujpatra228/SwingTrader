# Common Mistakes in Swing Trading

Every mistake below either costs money directly, or stops you from making money over time. These come from Ankur Patel's teachings — the same errors he sees beginners make repeatedly.

---

## Mistake 1: Skipping the market check (the most expensive mistake)

**What people do:** jump straight to finding stocks without checking NIFTY first.

**What happens:** you buy a breakout, NIFTY falls the next week, your stock falls with it, stop loss hit. You blame the stock. The real problem was the market.

**Ankur's rule:** 70% of stocks follow the market. If the market is falling, even the best stock will fall. Always check NIFTY first. If it is below its 20 EMA and 50 EMA, sit in cash. No exceptions.

**Fix:** make Step 1 a non-negotiable habit before opening any stock chart.

---

## Mistake 2: Buying a stock after it already ran up a lot

**What people do:** they see a stock up 10–15% today on the news, get excited, buy it.

**What happens:** they are buying when it is already extended — far away from its base, far above where the smart money bought. The stock is "tired." It reverses. They lose money.

**Real data from our backtest:** buying the day after a big volume spike → average loss of −0.88% even after 10 days. This is provably bad.

**Ankur's rule:** buy early in the trend, not after the big move is already done. The best profits come from catching a stock at the start of its move, not in the middle or at the end.

**Fix:** if a stock already ran 10%+ recently without resting, put it on a watchlist and wait. Let it form a new base. Then buy the breakout from that base.

---

## Mistake 3: Buying from the volume spike scan directly

**What people do:** run the volume scan, see stocks that jumped 6–10% today on high volume, buy them immediately.

**What happens:** same as Mistake 2. The scan shows stocks that just made a big move. That move is already done. Buying it now = buying extended.

**How the scan is actually meant to be used:** the volume scan finds stocks where big money stepped in. That stock now goes on your **watchlist**. You wait for it to rest and form a tight base (3–10 days of small moves). Then you buy the breakout from that base.

**Fix:** volume spike scan → watchlist → wait for base → buy the breakout. Never buy on the day of the spike.

## Mistake 4: No stop loss, or moving the stop loss down

**What people do (no stop loss):** buy a stock, it falls, they hold hoping it comes back. It keeps falling.

**What people do (moving stop down):** set a stop at ₹485, stock falls to ₹487, they think "it's so close, let me move the stop to ₹470 to give it more room." Stock falls to ₹450.

**What happens:** a small, manageable loss becomes a very large loss. One bad trade wipes out 3–5 good trades.

**Ankur's rule:** stop loss goes at the base low minus 0.5–1%. If price hits that level, the setup has failed — the thesis is dead. Exit immediately. No hoping. Never move the stop downward.

**The logic behind it:** the base low is the last point where buyers showed conviction. A break below it means those buyers are wrong — the stock is weaker than expected. Get out.

**Fix:** set the stop BEFORE you buy. Place it as an actual order in your broker app so it triggers automatically even if you are not watching.

---

## Mistake 5: Stop loss too tight

**What people do:** set a stop just 1–2% below entry, thinking they are being careful.

**What happens:** normal daily price movements (noise) hit the stop, knocking them out of trades that were actually fine. They miss the big move that comes after.

**Ankur's rule:** the stop must be at the level where the setup is *proven wrong* — which is the base low. Not at an arbitrary percentage. If the base is wide and the stop ends up being 8% away, then either accept that risk (with smaller position size) or skip the trade.

**Fix:** stop = base low minus 1%. If that distance feels too large, make the position smaller — do not move the stop closer.

---

## Mistake 6: Stop loss too loose

**What people do:** opposite of Mistake 5 — they set a stop 15–20% below entry, thinking "I'll give it room."

**What happens:** when the stop finally hits, the loss is enormous. A few of these wipe out months of gains.

**Fix:** stop always tied to the base low. If the base low is 15% below your entry — the stock is extended and should not be bought yet. Wait.

---

## Mistake 7: Cutting winners too early

**What people do:** stock is up 5%, they get nervous and sell to "lock in profits." Stock goes on to rise 25%.

**What happens:** their gains are tiny. Their losses (when stops hit) are the same size. Over time, the math does not work — small wins and normal-sized losses.

**Ankur's rule:** do not cut winners early to protect capital. That is the wrong fix. The right fix is position sizing — make the position small enough that you can comfortably hold through normal dips. Let the trade hit your target or get stopped out by a trailing stop, not by nervousness.

**Fix:** set your target (2× your risk) before you buy. Then let the trade run. Only exit early if: NIFTY is falling sharply, or the stock shows a clear climax (parabolic move — went up 20%+ in 2–3 days). Otherwise, hold.

## Mistake 8: Averaging down (buying more when the stock falls)

**What people do:** buy at ₹500, stock falls to ₹460, they buy more to "lower their average."

**What happens:** the stock was falling for a reason. It falls further. Now they have double the position in a losing trade. Loss doubles.

**Ankur's rule:** never average down. If the stock hit your stop loss level, the setup failed — exit, do not add more. Averaging down turns a small manageable loss into a large painful one.

**Fix:** if a stock falls to your stop level, sell. All of it. No adding. Look for a new setup.

---

## Mistake 9: Trading too many setups at the same time

**What people do:** try to trade VCP patterns, episodic pivots, reversal trades, EMA pullbacks, and flag breakouts all at once.

**What happens:** they are not good at any of them. Each setup requires different rules, different timing, different exit strategies. Trying all of them leads to confusion and losses.

**Ankur's rule:** pick ONE setup. Master it completely. Know every variation of it. Know exactly when it works and when it does not. Only after you are consistently profitable with one setup should you consider adding another.

**Fix:** as a beginner, pick just one — the tight base breakout (3-week tight close scan + Step 4 chart reading). Get very good at spotting it and trading it before adding anything else.

---

## Mistake 10: Ignoring which sector the stock is in

**What people do:** find a stock that looks good on a chart, buy it, without checking if its whole sector is going up or down.

**What happens:** even a great-looking stock will struggle if the entire sector is weak. For example, buying an IT stock when IT stocks across the board are falling — the sector is too heavy a weight for any individual stock to fight.

**Ankur's method:** market → sector → stock. Always in that order. Check which sectors are leading (use the Momentum scan — which sectors dominate it?). Buy stocks from the leading sectors. Avoid stocks from weak or falling sectors.

**Fix:** before adding any stock to your watchlist, check one thing — is the sector it belongs to trending upward? If other stocks in the same sector are also doing well, that is confirmation. If they are falling, skip it.

---

## Mistake 11: Not having a trading journal

**What people do:** trade, sometimes win, sometimes lose, but never write down what happened or why.

**What happens:** they repeat the same mistakes for months or years. They cannot improve because they have no record of what went wrong.

**Ankur's practice:** he keeps a journal of every trade — what the setup was, what went right, what went wrong. He has a video specifically on this: "My Trading Journal: How I Track Mistakes and Improve Faster."

**Fix:** for every trade, write down: date, stock, setup type, entry price, stop loss, target, reason for entry, and outcome. Review it weekly. Patterns of mistakes will emerge quickly.

---

## Mistake 12: Trading during bad market conditions

**What people do:** keep trying to find trades even when NIFTY is falling or choppy for weeks.

**What happens:** breakouts fail repeatedly. They get stopped out over and over. Confidence falls. They start breaking their own rules.

**Ankur's rule:** scale your exposure to match market conditions. When the market is clearly in a downtrend — go to cash, stop buying. When the market is choppy and uncertain — trade with very small position sizes or not at all. When the market is in a clear uptrend — that is when to be fully invested.

**He calls this:** moving from 0% invested to 100% invested and back to cash as conditions change. It is not about predicting the market. It is about reacting to what it is actually doing.

**Fix:** if NIFTY is below both the blue line (20 EMA) and green line (50 EMA) — stop buying new positions. Protect your capital. Better opportunities will come when the market recovers.
